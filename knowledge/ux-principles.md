# UX Principles — Applied Rules

How we apply named UX laws at Yoma Fleet. Cite the law by name in critiques and rationale.

## Laws as working rules

- **Jakob's Law**: users spend most time in other apps (Grab, banking apps, Facebook). Follow familiar regional patterns before inventing.
- **Fitts's Law**: primary actions get large targets in the thumb zone on mobile; destructive actions are placed away from primary ones.
- **Hick's Law**: reduce simultaneous choices; use progressive disclosure for long forms (Easy Lease applications especially).
- **Miller's Law**: chunk information; max ~5-7 items per visible group in navigation and dashboards.
- **Gestalt (proximity/similarity)**: spacing communicates grouping before borders or backgrounds do.
- **Peak-end rule**: invest in booking confirmation, approval, and trip-end moments; they define remembered experience.

## The 7 factors of UX (review lens)

Every screen review checks: useful, usable, findable, credible, desirable, accessible, valuable. A screen that fails "credible" (e.g. unclear pricing) fails review even if beautiful.

## Named patterns we use

Progressive disclosure, card grid, bottom sheet (mobile secondary actions), skeleton loading, empty states with next action, inline validation, save-progress for long forms.

## Non-negotiables

- WCAG 2.1 AA baseline
- Business goal AND user need stated before any pixel (no design in a vacuum)
- AI drafts, designer decides: every deliverable separates AI-suggested from designer-approved
